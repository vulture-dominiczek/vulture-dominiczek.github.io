Walking a maze

"How do you walk out of maze if you have unlimited number of stones?" This question from some book intriqued me lately. There is one to one relationship between mazes and graphs. You basically treat each cell as having four children links: north, west, south and east. If there is an passege then there is connection between the cells via any one of the children links. Or more compact version: nodes are junctions and passages are the edges (we skip simple passthrough cells). In this post I will describe algorithmic ways of solving the puzzle.

Simple maze

This is a "simple maze":

Or scientifically: simply connected or perfect maze. It is a maze that is topologically equivalent to circle. But what does that mean? topological part means that you can take some surface and apply any given transformations to it's edges as long as you don't "cut" the original piece along the way. This way a cup is topologically equivalent to doughnut.

In this case you don't need any stones at all to leave the maze! You just follow left hand rule of following the maze. It states: to leave the maze keep your hand touching left wall all the time and go straight swerving on the corners, then you eventually hit the exit. And that's it. Just because the maze is in reality rugged circle.

Hard maze

What if you have other type of maze? If it has loops then you will keep going same path again and again. Now you have to consequently checkout every pathway and mark already visited. This is where stones will come in handy.

So how about a following method:

  • Mark each our step with a stone (you are leaving stones while walking).
  • On junction visit a corridor that is not marked by a stone
  • If you reached dead end (or junction marked with stone) back up leaving 2 stones until closest junction
  • Pick any corridor without stones if not possible back up until next junction leaving 2 stones.

This simple method guarantees finding the exit eventually (not neccessarily shortest one though).

This is slightly modified version of Trémaux's algorithm invented in 19th century.

But it is nothing more than Depth First Search. Who knows maybe it's descendant of Trémaux method.
